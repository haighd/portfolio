## The Path Wasn't Straight

If you're expecting a tidy story about climbing the corporate ladder one rung at a time, this isn't it. My path from individual contributor to Director involved lateral moves, industry hops, and at least one title that looked like a step backward on paper.

I started in Six Sigma and continuous improvement - not as a data scientist or analyst, but as someone obsessed with process optimization and measurable outcomes. That foundation shaped everything that came after.

### What Actually Changes

**As an IC**, success meant:
- Delivering high-quality analysis
- Solving complex problems
- Being the expert in the room

**As a leader**, success meant:
- Enabling others to deliver
- Creating clarity from ambiguity
- Building systems that scale beyond you

That role led to an Associate Director position in Supply Chain Analytics, where I pivoted deeper into data science and ML applications. The Six Sigma foundation met modern analytics tooling.

### Lessons from Lateral Moves

1. **Titles are lagging indicators** - Your skills and impact grow before your title catches up
2. **Domain expertise compounds** - Each industry taught me something that made me better in the next
3. **Networks transfer** - The relationships you build matter more than the org chart position

## Building and Coaching Teams

The work of leadership is people work. At Merck, I've led teams from 12 data scientists to now 6 Associate Directors. The scale changed, but the fundamentals didn't.

### What I've Learned About Developing People

**Career conversations matter more than performance reviews.** The best conversations I have with direct reports aren't about their current project - they're about where they want to be in three years and what's blocking them.

{/* TODO: Add example of a coaching win - someone you helped grow */}

**Create systems, not dependencies.** Early on, I was the answer to every question. That doesn't scale. Now I focus on documentation, templates, and standard work that lets the team operate without me in the room.

**Give people the problem, not the solution.** The instinct is to tell people what to do. The better approach is to be clear about the outcome and let them find the path.

## The Strategic Shift

Director-level work is less about data and more about decisions. My days now involve:

- Removing blockers across teams
- Translating technical work into business impact for stakeholders
- Making resource allocation tradeoffs
- Developing the next generation of leaders

The technical skills that got me here still matter - I still review dashboards, discuss model approaches, and care deeply about methodology. But they're table stakes now, not the main event.

## What I'd Tell My IC Self

1. **Start managing up earlier** - Learn to communicate impact in business terms, not technical terms
2. **Build relationships outside your function** - The cross-functional network becomes essential later
3. **Document everything** - Future you (and your team) will thank you
4. **Volunteer for ambiguous problems** - The messy, undefined projects are where leadership skills develop
5. **Find sponsors, not just mentors** - You need people who advocate for you in rooms you're not in

## The Uncomfortable Truth

Leadership isn't for everyone, and that's fine. Some of the most valuable people I've worked with are senior ICs who chose depth over management. They're not "stuck" - they made a deliberate choice.

If you want leadership because you think it's the only way to advance, examine that assumption. If you want it because you genuinely enjoy enabling others and building systems that scale, you might be ready.
